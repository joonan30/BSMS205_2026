Chapter 27 · A case study in cis-regulatory therapy
Welcome to Chapter twenty-seven, CRISPR a Therapy for S C N two A Haploinsufficiency. In the previous chapter we saw the modern reverse genetics toolkit, including CRISPR a — the system that uses a dead Cas nine fused to a transcriptional activator to boost gene expression. Today we follow that tool from the lab bench all the way toward the clinic, applied to one specific severe disorder. This is a real story. The paper came out in twenty twenty-five, in Nature.
Today's central question

Can you treat a genetic disease
by simply turning up the volume
on the patient's normal allele?

Here is the central question. For many genetic disorders, one allele has a damaging mutation, but the other allele is completely normal. The problem is not the bad allele — it is that one good allele is not making enough protein on its own. So instead of fixing the bad allele, what if we just turned up the volume on the good one? Could that be a therapy? Today's chapter says yes — and we will see exactly how.

The therapeutic concept

Don't replace the gene.
Don't edit the mutation.
Boost the remaining normal allele.

This is called dosage restoration therapy.

Here is the new therapeutic concept. For decades, genetic therapies fell into two categories. Gene replacement — add a new working copy. Gene editing — fix the broken copy. Both rewrite the genome. The Tamura twenty twenty-five paper demonstrates a third path. Don't replace. Don't edit. Just boost the existing normal allele. The patient already has a good copy. We just need it to work harder. This idea is called dosage restoration therapy, and it is now a real therapeutic strategy in development.

§ 1

SCN2A and the
Haploinsufficiency Problem

Let's start with the gene itself.

SCN2A · a sodium channel

  • Encodes NaV1.2 · voltage-gated sodium channel
  • Critical for action potential generation
  • Especially important in early brain development
  • Highly expressed in excitatory neurons
S C N two A encodes a protein called Na V one point two — a voltage-gated sodium channel. When a neuron fires an action potential, sodium channels open and let sodium ions rush into the cell, depolarising the membrane and propagating the electrical signal. Na V one point two is especially important in early brain development and in certain types of excitatory cortical neurons. Without enough of it, neurons cannot fire normally.

Haploinsufficiency · one copy is not enough

Normal

  • Two functional alleles
  • 50% + 50% = 100% protein
  • Neurons fire normally

Haploinsufficient

  • One functional · one LoF
  • 50% + 0% = 50% protein
  • Neurons under-fire
Haploinsufficiency means that one functional copy is not enough for normal function. In a healthy person, both alleles contribute about fifty percent of the protein, totalling one hundred percent. In a haploinsufficient patient, one allele has a loss-of-function mutation and produces zero protein. The remaining good allele produces fifty percent. That is half the normal level — and for some genes, like S C N two A, fifty percent is not enough. Neurons become under-excitable. They cannot fire properly. The result is severe disease.

Clinical consequences of SCN2A LoF

  • Developmental delay
  • Epilepsy — including paradoxical seizures
  • Autism spectrum disorder
  • Intellectual disability
The clinical consequences of S C N two A loss-of-function variants are severe. Patients show developmental delay, often noticed within the first year of life. They develop epilepsy — paradoxically, because losing an excitatory channel can also weaken inhibitory circuits, leading to seizures. Many patients also have autism spectrum disorder and intellectual disability. The combination is sometimes called developmental and epileptic encephalopathy. There is currently no targeted therapy. Standard antiepileptic drugs help only modestly.

The key insight

The remaining SCN2A allele is perfectly normal.
It just isn't producing enough protein.

Therapeutic question: can we make the good copy work harder?

And here is the insight that motivates today's chapter. In an S C N two A haploinsufficient patient, the surviving allele is perfectly normal. There is nothing wrong with it. It is producing protein at the normal rate for one allele. The problem is just that one allele's worth of output is not enough. So the therapeutic question becomes: can we make the good copy work harder? Can we double its output? If yes, we restore the patient to roughly normal protein levels — a hundred percent — without ever touching the broken allele.
§ 2

Not All Variants
Are the Same

Before we get to therapy, an important diagnostic point. Not all S C N two A variants are equivalent.

Two opposite variant classes

ClassChannelNeuronPhenotype
Loss of functionReduced or absentHypoexcitableDevelopmental delay · ASD
Gain of functionExcessive · prolonged openingHyperexcitableInfantile spasms · early epilepsy
S C N two A variants split into two opposite classes. Loss of function variants — nonsense, frameshift, splice-disrupting, or destabilising missense — reduce or eliminate channel activity. Neurons become hypoexcitable, leading to developmental delay and autism. Gain-of-function variants — typically missense in the voltage sensor or inactivation gate — cause channels to open too easily or stay open too long. Neurons become hyperexcitable, leading to infantile spasms and severe early-onset epilepsy. The two classes need opposite therapies. CRISPR a — boosting expression — is appropriate only for the loss-of-function class.

Why the distinction matters for therapy

  • LoF haploinsufficiency · neurons need more NaV1.2 → use CRISPRa
  • GoF · neurons have too much activity → would need CRISPRi or sodium channel blocker
  • Genetic diagnosis must distinguish the two classes
The diagnostic distinction is critical. If a patient has loss of function and you boost S C N two A with CRISPR a, you restore them to normal. If you mistakenly boost a gain-of-function patient, you make them worse — pushing already over-excitable channels even higher. So accurate variant classification has to come first. The good news is that current functional assays in induced pluripotent stem cell-derived neurons can usually distinguish the two classes. Today's discussion focuses on the loss-of-function group.
§ 3

How CRISPRa Works

Now let's understand the mechanism of CRISPR a.

Standard CRISPR · cuts DNA

  • Guide RNA targets the protein
  • Cas9 cuts both strands
  • Cell repairs the break, often with errors
  • Result: permanent DNA change
Recall the standard CRISPR system. A guide R N A directs the Cas nine protein to a specific D N A sequence. Cas nine cuts both strands. The cell repairs the break, often with errors. The end result is a permanent change to the genome. That is great for knocking out genes or correcting mutations. But for haploinsufficiency, we do not want to change the D N A. We want to change the expression. So we use a different version.

CRISPRa · activates without cutting

  • dCas9 — catalytically dead · binds DNA but cannot cut
  • Fused to activator domain (e.g. VPR = VP64 + p65 + Rta)
  • Guide to gene's promoter → recruits transcription machinery
  • More mRNA · more protein · DNA unchanged
CRISPR a uses a modified version of Cas nine called d Cas nine — dead Cas nine — that has been engineered to bind D N A but not cut. By itself, d Cas nine does little useful. But fused to a transcriptional activator domain — for example, V P R, which combines V P sixty-four, p sixty-five, and R t a — it becomes a powerful gene activator. Guide it to a target gene's promoter, and it recruits R N A polymerase and the rest of the transcription machinery, boosting expression. The D N A sequence is unchanged. The cell just makes more m R N A and more protein. Think of it as a volume knob.

The volume knob mental model

Standard CRISPR

  • Permanent DNA edit
  • Like rewriting the score

CRISPRa

  • Reversible expression change
  • Like turning up the volume
Here is the useful mental model. Standard CRISPR is like rewriting the musical score itself — the change is permanent and propagates indefinitely. CRISPR a is like turning up the volume knob on the amplifier. The score stays the same. The output increases. Stop the system, and the output goes back down. This reversibility is one of the key safety advantages of CRISPR a as a therapy.
§ 4

The Tamura 2025 Study

Now let's look at the actual experiments.

Experimental design

  1. Generate Scn2a haploinsufficient mice (one WT + one LoF allele)
  2. Design CRISPRa: dCas9-VPR + guide RNA targeting Scn2a promoter
  3. Package into AAV9 · deliver systemically
  4. Treat adolescent mice (P30–P60)
  5. Measure: mRNA · sodium current · firing · seizures · behaviour

Tamura et al. 2025, Nature

The experimental design has five steps. One. Generate mice carrying one wild-type Scn2a allele and one loss-of-function allele. These mice show reduced sodium currents, abnormal E E G, and behavioural deficits — a faithful model of human haploinsufficiency. Two. Design the CRISPR a system — d Cas nine fused to V P R, plus a guide R N A targeting the Scn2a promoter. Three. Package everything into an A A V nine viral vector for delivery. Four. Inject systemically into adolescent mice, postnatal day thirty to sixty. Five. Measure outcomes at the molecular, cellular, circuit, and behavioural levels.

Result 1 · expression restored

Here is the molecular result. The left panel shows m R N A levels. Wild-type mice are at one hundred percent. Untreated heterozygotes are at about fifty percent — half the normal level, exactly as expected for haploinsufficiency. After CRISPR a treatment, m R N A jumps back up to roughly eighty to ninety percent. Nearly complete restoration. The right panel shows the functional consequence — sodium current amplitude in patch-clamp recordings. Same story: from fifty percent up to eighty-five percent of normal. Boosting m R N A directly translates into more functional channels at the neuronal membrane.

Result 2 · firing rescued

Here is the cellular result that matters most. Each panel shows action potentials recorded from a single neuron in response to current injection. Wild-type neurons in black fire many fast spikes. Heterozygous neurons in cyan fire much less — they are sluggish, with only half the normal sodium channel density. After CRISPR a treatment, the heterozygous neurons in purple fire just like wild-type. Firing rescue is essentially complete. This is the cellular proof of concept — restore the protein, restore the function.
§ 5

Behavior and Seizures

Cellular rescue is encouraging. But we want to know whether it translates to the whole animal.

Result 3 · seizure protection

Here is the most clinically meaningful result. The mice were challenged with four-aminopyridine, a chemoconvulsant that triggers seizures by blocking potassium channels. The graph shows survival over sixty minutes. Wild-type mice in black do well. Untreated heterozygous mice in cyan are highly vulnerable — about sixty percent die from severe tonic-clonic seizures within an hour. But CRISPR a-treated heterozygous mice in purple survive at nearly the wild-type rate. This is not just a lab measurement. Seizures are the most dangerous symptom of S C N two A haploinsufficiency in human patients. CRISPR a provides clinically meaningful protection.

Result 4 · behavioural improvement

  • Synaptic transmission · restored in cortical pyramidal cells
  • Social interaction · improved
  • Overall phenotype · near-normal
  • Treatment in adolescence · still works
Beyond seizure protection, the treated mice showed improvements at the synaptic and behavioural levels. Excitatory synaptic transmission in cortical pyramidal cells was restored. Social interaction tests improved. The overall phenotype moved from disease state to near-normal. And critically — the treatment was given in adolescence, long after early brain development. That is a remarkable finding. It suggests that the therapeutic window for S C N two A intervention is not limited to early infancy. Patients diagnosed later in childhood may still benefit. That dramatically broadens the clinical relevance of this approach.

Also tested in human cells

  • Human iPSC-derived neurons with SCN2A LoF
  • CRISPRa boosted SCN2A expression
  • Sodium currents recovered
  • Approach is not mouse-specific
The Tamura paper also tested CRISPR a in human induced pluripotent stem cell-derived neurons carrying an S C N two A loss-of-function variant. The same boost in expression and the same recovery of sodium currents was observed. This is critical because it shows the approach is not mouse-specific — it works in human cells. That is a necessary stepping stone toward clinical translation.
§ 6

Why This Matters ·
Cis-Regulatory Therapy

Let's step back and put this in a broader context.

Five advantages of CRISPRa therapy

  • No DNA cutting — no risk of off-target indels or rearrangements
  • Endogenous regulation — gene stays under native promoter and enhancers
  • Tunable and reversible — adjust dose · stop if needed
  • Mutation-agnostic — works for any LoF as long as one allele is intact
  • Late intervention possible — adolescent mice still rescued
CRISPR a therapy has five major advantages over alternatives. One. No D N A cutting, so no off-target indels, no chromosomal rearrangements. Two. Endogenous regulation — the activated gene is still under its native promoter and enhancers, so expression remains responsive to normal developmental and physiological signals. Three. The effect is tunable and reversible. You can adjust the dose. If problems arise, you can stop. Compare that to a permanent gene edit. Four. Mutation-agnostic. The same CRISPR a system works for any loss-of-function variant — nonsense, frameshift, splice — as long as the other allele is intact. You do not need a different therapy for each mutation. And five, the most surprising finding — the late therapeutic window.

How CRISPRa compares to alternatives

StrategyWhat it doesTrade-off
Gene replacementAdd a new SCN2A copy via AAVSCN2A is too large for AAV (6 kb)
Base editingCorrect point mutationsOnly works for specific mutation types
Small moleculesModulate channel activityNon-specific · affects all Na channels
CRISPRaBoost normal alleleRequires viral delivery · long-term safety TBD
Let's compare CRISPR a to other therapeutic strategies. Gene replacement, the classic approach, packages a fresh S C N two A copy into A A V. The trouble is that S C N two A is about six kilobases, larger than the four point seven kilobase A A V capacity. You need split vectors or trimmed promoters, both of which compromise expression control. Base editing can correct specific point mutations but does not work for all variant types — frameshifts, splice mutations, large deletions are all out of reach. Small molecule sodium channel modulators exist but are not selective for Na V one point two — they also hit the other sodium channel subtypes, with side effects. CRISPR a is the only approach that is both mutation-agnostic and dosage-restoring. The trade-offs are viral delivery and long-term persistence, which still need to be characterised.

The bigger picture · cis-regulatory gene therapy

  • SCN1A in Dravet syndrome · CRISPRa development active
  • SIM1 in obesity from haploinsufficiency · CRISPRa works in mice
  • HTT in Huntington's · CRISPRi to silence repeat expansion
  • Hundreds of haploinsufficiency disorders are candidates
CRISPR a for S C N two A is part of a broader trend called cis-regulatory gene therapy. Other examples are already in development. CRISPR a for S C N one A in Dravet syndrome — another sodium channelopathy. CRISPR a for S I M one in obesity caused by haploinsufficiency. CRISPR i for the H T T repeat expansion in Huntington's disease, where you want to silence rather than activate. The unifying principle is to adjust gene expression to restore balance, rather than rewrite the genome. Hundreds of haploinsufficiency disorders are candidates for this strategy — Shank three in Phelan-McDermid syndrome, M E C P two in Rett syndrome, F O X P one in intellectual disability, and many others. If CRISPR a for S C N two A reaches the clinic successfully, the door opens for all of them.
